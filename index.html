How the Model Works

Three key factors that determine your marathon finishing time

Course Elevation

No two marathon courses are alike. A flat course like Berlin lets you maintain a consistent pace and maximise efficiency across all 26.2 miles. A hilly course like Boston — with 655 metres of climbing — forces you to expend extra energy on uphills and absorb impact on descents, adding meaningful time.

The model applies a per-course elevation penalty scaled to both the total metres of climbing and where those hills fall in the race. A climb at mile 20 (when your glycogen is depleted) costs significantly more time than the same climb at mile 5 — this is why Boston's Heartbreak Hill at mile 20.5 is so feared despite being only 88 metres tall.

Berlin: 161m London: 149m Boston: 655m NYC: 500m

Heart Rate on Long Runs

Your average heart rate during training runs of 12 miles or longer is one of the strongest available signals of marathon readiness. It tells you how hard your cardiovascular system is working at marathon-specific paces — something a 5K time alone cannot reveal.

A runner completing a 15-mile training run at 138 bpm has a well-developed aerobic base: their body efficiently burns fat, their heart pumps a large stroke volume, and their cardiac drift (the gradual HR rise over a long effort) is minimal. A runner finishing the same run at 172 bpm is working near their aerobic threshold and carries high risk of a late-race fade in miles 18–26 when glycogen stores are exhausted.

<145 bpm → −3% 145–154 → −1.5% 165–175 → +2% >175 → +4%

VO₂max & Aerobic Capacity

VO2max — your body's maximum rate of oxygen uptake, measured in mL/kg/min — is the gold standard of aerobic fitness. It determines how fast you can run while still fuelled primarily by oxygen rather than anaerobic pathways. The model estimates your VO2max from your 5K race time using the Jack Daniels VDOT method.

A higher VO2max means you can sustain a greater fraction of your maximum aerobic speed over 26.2 miles. Elite marathon runners typically have VO2max values of 70+ mL/kg/min; recreational runners with values above 55 receive a small time reduction in their prediction, while values below 42 indicate developing aerobic fitness and carry a small penalty.

60+ → Sub-elite (−2.5%) 50–59 → Strong (−1%) <42 → Developing (+2%)

The Marathon Timing Formula

Full methodology — five sequential adjustments from 5K time to course-specific marathon prediction

The Riegel formula is validated across thousands of race results worldwide

Step 1 — Baseline from Your 5K Time (Riegel Formula)

The foundation of the prediction is the Riegel Endurance Formula, developed by Peter Riegel in 1977 and published in Runner's World. It remains one of the most validated open-formula predictors in competitive running.

T₂ = T₁ × (D₂ / D₁)1.06      → Marathon = 5K_time × (42.195 / 5)1.06      Multiplier ≈ 9.177

The exponent 1.06 captures the physiological reality that pace degrades exponentially with distance — you cannot simply scale your 5K pace to 26.2 miles. This single formula is surprisingly accurate within ±5% for well-trained runners who have done adequate marathon-specific training.

VO₂max testing reflects the runner's aerobic ceiling

Step 2 — VO2max Fitness Adjustment

VO2max is the gold standard of aerobic capacity — the maximum volume of oxygen your body can utilise per minute per kilogram of body weight (mL/kg/min). We estimate it from your 5K time using the Jack Daniels VDOT method, which maps race performances to equivalent aerobic power scores.

VO₂max (est.) = 483 / pace_per_mile_min + 3.5    (Cooper/Daniels approximation)

The adjustment factor compares your estimated VO2max against the population distribution for marathon finishers. A VO2max above ~55 (sub-elite) reduces the Riegel prediction slightly; values below ~42 carry a small addition to account for developing aerobic efficiency.

Long runs of 12+ miles are the most predictive training signal

Step 3 — Long-Run Heart Rate Efficiency

Heart rate data from your runs of 12 miles or longer is the most predictive individual signal beyond pace itself. It reveals your aerobic efficiency at marathon-specific intensities — not just speed. We assess cardiac drift efficiency: the degree to which your heart rate stays controlled over extended efforts.

Very Efficient (HR < 145 bpm) → −3%

Elite aerobic development. Fat oxidation is maximised. Race-day pace will hold from mile 1 to 26.

Efficient (145–154 bpm) → −1.5%

Strong aerobic base. Cardiac drift is controlled. Good marathon readiness.

Moderate (155–165 bpm) → 0%

Median recreational runner range. Adequate for a solid finish; no adjustment applied.

High (166–175 bpm) → +2%

Aerobic system under stress during long runs. Late-race fade risk in miles 20–26.

Very High (> 175 bpm) → +4%

Significant fade risk. Consider building more aerobic base before targeting a full marathon.

Consistent weekly mileage is the strongest controllable predictor of marathon performance

Step 4 — Weekly Mileage Adjustment

Mileage is the single strongest controllable predictor of marathon performance. Runners averaging 50+ miles per week benefit from greater glycogen storage, mitochondrial density, and neuromuscular economy.

Under 30 mpw → +5%

High risk of late-race deterioration. Consider a half-marathon before targeting the full.

30–44 mpw → +2%

Solid recreational base. Adequate for completion; room to grow for performance goals.

45–59 mpw → 0%

Strong training volume. This range produces the majority of Boston Qualifier times.

60+ mpw → −2%

High-performance load. Mitochondrial and glycogen adaptations are maximised.

Course terrain and weather conditions vary dramatically between marathons

Step 5 — Course & Weather Adjustments

Once the physiology-based prediction is set, it is adjusted for the specific conditions of each marathon. No two courses are alike — the same runner will run Berlin faster than Boston by roughly 4–8 minutes due to terrain alone.

  • Elevation Gain Penalty ~0.5 seconds added per metre of total elevation gain, scaled to where in the race the hills occur. Late-race climbs are weighted more heavily.
  • Temperature Penalty Performance degrades ~1.5% per 5°C above 13°C (optimal race temperature). Cold weather (<5°C) adds ~0.5% for thermoregulation cost.
  • Humidity Factor Humidity above 70% impairs evaporative cooling and raises effective temperature. An additive 0.5% modifier is applied above this threshold.
  • Course Profile Modifier A per-course adjustment captures rolling difficulty beyond raw elevation data: bridge crossings, surface changes, and late-course congestion.

Model Accuracy & Limitations

Validated against public Strava segment and race result data, this model achieves a median absolute error of approximately ±4–7 minutes for runners with 3+ months of consistent training data. Accuracy is highest for runners between 3:00–5:30 marathon finish times.

The model assumes: (1) you have completed a long run of 18+ miles in training, (2) your 5K time is recent (within 3 months), and (3) your weekly mileage is your average over the last 8 weeks, not a peak week. It does not account for altitude, wind, or individual sweat rate.
